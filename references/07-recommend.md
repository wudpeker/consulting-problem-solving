# Step 7: Develop Recommendations

The synthesis told us what's happening and why. Now we need to tell the stakeholder what to do about it. Good recommendations are specific, actionable, prioritized, and evidence-backed.

## Interaction Model: User Provides Strategic Input and Selects Among Options

The partner shapes the strategic direction and makes the final call on which recommendations to develop. You present options with trade-offs; they choose. You draft the details; they refine. This is not a step where you hand down "the answer" — it's where you and the partner co-develop the recommendation together.

### INPUT Phase: Get Strategic Preferences and Constraints

Before developing recommendations, ask the user:

1. **"What strategic direction are you leaning toward?"** — The partner may already have a preferred path. If so, build around it.
2. **"Are there options that are off the table?"** — Organizational, political, or practical constraints may eliminate certain recommendation directions before you start.
3. **"What's your organization's appetite for risk and change?"** — This shapes how bold the recommendations should be.
4. **"Who needs to approve these recommendations? What do they care about?"** — The approval pathway shapes the framing.
5. **"What's the budget and timeline for implementation?"** — Grounds the recommendations in reality.
6. **"Are there past initiatives or attempts that we should reference or learn from?"** — Organizational memory matters.

Incorporate their answers into the recommendation development. If the partner has a clear strategic preference, lead with it.

## What You're Producing

A **Recommendation Brief** (.md) containing:

1. **Core recommendation**: The headline action(s)
2. **Supporting recommendations**: Enabling actions
3. **Implementation roadmap**: Phasing and sequencing
4. **Expected impact**: Quantified where possible
5. **Risks and mitigations**: What could go wrong and how to handle it

## The SMART+E Test

Every recommendation should pass this test:

- **Specific**: "Reduce SKU count by 30% in the low-margin product lines" not "simplify the portfolio"
- **Measurable**: Include target metrics and timeframes
- **Actionable**: The stakeholder must be able to act on it with existing or obtainable resources
- **Relevant**: Directly addresses the key question and is supported by the analysis
- **Time-bound**: Clear milestones and deadlines
- **Evidence-backed**: You can point to specific findings from Step 5 that support this recommendation

## Structuring Recommendations

### The Pyramid

```
Core Recommendation (the big move)
├── Supporting Recommendation A (enables the core)
│   ├── Specific action A1
│   └── Specific action A2
├── Supporting Recommendation B
│   ├── Specific action B1
│   └── Specific action B2
└── Supporting Recommendation C
    ├── Specific action C1
    └── Specific action C2
```

### Phasing

Almost no recommendation should be "do everything at once." Organize into phases:

- **Immediate (0-3 months)**: Quick wins, no-regret moves, foundations
- **Near-term (3-12 months)**: Major initiatives, organizational changes
- **Medium-term (1-3 years)**: Structural changes, capability building
- **Long-term (3+ years)**: Vision, strategic positioning

Each phase should deliver value even if later phases don't happen.

## Quantifying Impact

For each recommendation, estimate:

- **Financial impact**: Revenue uplift, cost savings, margin improvement
- **Effort required**: Investment, resources, time
- **Payback period**: When does the investment pay for itself?
- **Confidence**: Express as a range (e.g., "$5-8M annual savings")

## Presenting Options to the User

Before finalizing, present 2-3 distinct strategic directions with trade-offs:

**Option A: [Name — e.g., "Bold transformation"]**
- What it involves: [Summary]
- Pros: [Key advantages]
- Cons: [Key risks/costs]
- Expected impact: [Range]

**Option B: [Name — e.g., "Focused optimization"]**
- What it involves: [Summary]
- Pros: [Key advantages]
- Cons: [Key risks/costs]
- Expected impact: [Range]

**Option C: [Name — e.g., "Phased approach"]**
- What it involves: [Summary]
- Pros: [Key advantages]
- Cons: [Key risks/costs]
- Expected impact: [Range]

**Your recommendation**: "I'd lean toward Option [X] because [reasoning]. But the choice depends on [factors the user knows better than you]. Which direction resonates?"

## Addressing "Why Not?"

For every recommendation, anticipate objections:

| Objection | Response |
|-----------|----------|
| "We tried this before" | [What's different now] |
| "Too risky" | [Risk mitigation plan, evidence from benchmarks] |
| "Too expensive" | [ROI analysis, phased approach to manage investment] |
| "Politically difficult" | [Stakeholder management plan, quick wins to build momentum] |
| "We don't have the capabilities" | [Build, buy, or partner plan] |

## REVIEW Phase: User Selects and Refines Recommendations

Present the options and the draft recommendation brief for the user's explicit approval.

1. **Present options clearly**: "Here are the strategic directions I see, with trade-offs. Which resonates with you? Or is there a direction I haven't considered?"
2. **Let the user choose**: Wait for them to select a direction. Don't proceed until they've chosen.
3. **After selection, present the draft brief**: "Based on your choice of [Option X], here's the full recommendation brief. Does this capture it correctly?"
4. **Validate feasibility**: "The implementation would require [resources]. Is that realistic? Any barriers I should factor in?"
5. **Co-develop phasing**: "Here's my suggested phasing. Does this sequencing make sense for your organization?"
6. **Validate impact estimates**: "I'm estimating impact at [range]. Does that feel directionally right?"
7. **Anticipate objections together**: "When you present this, what pushback do you expect? Who might resist?" — Build their answers into the objection-handling section.
8. **Confirm the core recommendation**: "So we're going with: [core recommendation]. Confirmed?"

**Do not proceed to Step 8 until the user explicitly approves the recommendations.** If they want to change direction, add recommendations, or adjust phasing, revise and re-present.

## Common Pitfalls

- **Vague recommendations**: "Improve customer experience" is an aspiration, not a recommendation. Get specific.
- **Recommendations not supported by evidence**: Every recommendation must trace back to analysis findings.
- **Too many recommendations**: Stakeholders can absorb 3-5 major recommendations. Consolidate.
- **No implementation path**: A recommendation without a roadmap is a wish.
- **Ignoring organizational reality**: Technically optimal recommendations that ignore politics, culture, or capability constraints will fail.
- **Presenting a single option**: Always give the user choices. They know their organization better than any framework.
- **Pushing your preferred option**: Present options fairly. Recommend one, but respect the user's choice even if it's not your top pick.
