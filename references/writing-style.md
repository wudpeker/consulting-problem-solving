# Writing Style Guide: Consulting-Grade Prose

This reference defines the writing standard for all deliverables produced by this skill. The principles are drawn from Strunk & White's *The Elements of Style* and adapted for consulting output. Read this before writing any final deliverable — especially the synthesis document, recommendation brief, and communication output (Step 8).

The goal: prose that is clear, tight, and forceful. Every sentence earns its place. No filler, no fluff, no hedging.

---

## The 10 Core Principles

Apply these in roughly this order of impact.

### 1. Omit needless words

The single most important principle. Vigorous writing is concise. A sentence should contain no unnecessary words, a paragraph no unnecessary sentences. This does not mean all sentences must be short — it means every word must work.

Common offenders:

| Wordy | Concise |
|-------|---------|
| the question as to whether | whether |
| owing to the fact that | since / because |
| in spite of the fact that | though / although |
| call your attention to the fact that | remind you |
| the fact that he had not succeeded | his failure |
| there is no doubt but that | doubtless |
| used for fuel purposes | used for fuel |
| in a hasty manner | hastily |
| this is a subject that | this subject |
| the reason why is that | because |

Also watch for:
- **"The fact that"**: Revise it out of every sentence where it appears.
- **Expletive constructions**: "There is/are..." and "It is...that..." delay the real subject. "There are many factors that suggest..." → "Several factors suggest..."
- **Redundant pairs**: "each and every", "first and foremost", "various and sundry"
- **Hollow intensifiers**: "very", "really", "extremely", "quite", "rather" — these almost never add meaning. Cut them.
- **Throat-clearing openers**: "It should be noted that...", "It is important to recognize that..." — delete and start with the actual point.

### 2. Use the active voice

The active voice is direct and forceful:
- Active: "Revenue declined 12% in Q3."
- Passive: "A 12% decline in revenue was observed in Q3."

The passive is fine when:
- The actor is unknown: "The data was collected in 2019."
- The actor is irrelevant: "The regulation was enacted last year."
- You need to emphasize the receiver: "The CEO was terminated."

But these are deliberate choices, not defaults. If you can't articulate why a sentence is passive, make it active.

### 3. Put statements in positive form

Say what something *is*, not what it *isn't*:
- "He usually came late" — not "He was not very often on time"
- "She thought the study was flawed" — not "She did not think the study was reliable"
- "Revenue declined" — not "Revenue did not grow"

Hedging with negatives ("not unlike", "not without merit") weakens prose.

### 4. Use definite, specific, concrete language

Prefer the specific to the general, the definite to the vague, the concrete to the abstract:
- "$4.2M in annual savings" — not "significant cost reduction"
- "Revenue fell 12% in Q3, driven by enterprise deal slippage" — not "Revenue experienced a period of unfavorable performance"
- "Cut headcount by 15% in the operations division" — not "Implement organizational optimization initiatives"

In consulting deliverables, this means: use numbers, name the segments, specify the timeframes. Vague language in a recommendation is a sign that the analysis isn't finished.

### 5. Keep related words together

Modifiers should sit next to what they modify. Subjects and verbs should stay close:
- Unclear: "The proposal, which was submitted last Tuesday and which the committee reviewed at length during a meeting that ran three hours over schedule, was rejected."
- Clear: "The committee rejected the proposal submitted last Tuesday, after a review that ran three hours over schedule."

### 6. Place emphatic words at the end

The end of a sentence is its most prominent position:
- Weak: "In many other ways, the business has improved since the restructuring."
- Strong: "Since the restructuring, the business has improved in many other ways."

For slide titles and key messages, put the punch at the end.

### 7. Avoid fancy words

Anglo-Saxon words usually beat Latinate ones. Simple language is not simple thinking — it is clear communication.

| Fancy | Plain |
|-------|-------|
| utilize | use |
| facilitate | help |
| commence | start / begin |
| terminate | end |
| finalize | finish / complete |
| operationalize | implement / run |
| incentivize | reward / encourage |
| leverage (as verb) | use |
| optimize | improve |
| prioritize | rank / focus on |

Write with nouns and verbs, not adjectives and adverbs. Choose precise nouns ("surgeon" not "medical professional") and vivid verbs ("costs surged" not "costs experienced increases").

### 8. Do not overwrite

Rich, elaborate prose is hard to pull off. When in doubt, cut back. Overwriting shows up as piled-on adjectives, strained metaphors, and breathless phrasing:
- Overwritten: "This transformative, game-changing initiative will fundamentally revolutionize..."
- Better: "This initiative will change how..."

Good consulting prose is confident and restrained.

### 9. Do not overstate

Overstatement breeds mistrust — fatal in a consulting context where credibility is everything:
- "The most devastating crisis in the company's history" — is it?
- "This will completely eliminate all risk" — will it?

Understatement is more persuasive. State facts. Let the evidence carry the weight. Use ranges rather than false precision.

### 10. Write naturally

The best writing sounds like a person talking clearly. If a sentence feels stiff or awkward when read aloud, rewrite it. Consulting prose should be precise without being stuffy.

## Consulting-Specific Style Rules

These rules supplement Strunk & White for the specific context of consulting deliverables.

### Slide titles must be complete sentences

Every slide title states the key message — not a topic label:
- Bad: "Market Analysis"
- Good: "The target market is growing at 12% annually, driven by segment X"

The title is the "so what?" of the slide. The body is the evidence.

### Lead with the answer

In every deliverable — slides, memos, sections — state the conclusion first, then support it. Don't make the reader wade through the reasoning to find the point.

### One message per unit

One message per slide. One idea per paragraph. One recommendation per section. If a slide has two messages, make it two slides.

### Source everything

Every data point in a deliverable needs a source. Even estimates need to be labeled as such with the basis stated.

### Be precise about uncertainty

Don't hide uncertainty — flag it clearly:
- "We estimate $4-6M in savings (medium confidence; based on proxy benchmarks)"
- Not: "There could be significant savings"

## Words and Expressions to Avoid

These commonly appear in consulting prose and should be flagged for revision:

| Expression | Problem | Better |
|------------|---------|--------|
| "In terms of" | Padding | Cut and restructure |
| "At the end of the day" | Cliche | Cut |
| "Going forward" | Usually redundant | Cut or use "next" |
| "Key learnings" | "Learnings" is jargon | "Lessons" or "findings" |
| "Synergies" | Vague | Name the specific benefit |
| "Best-in-class" | Vague superlative | "Top-quartile" or name the benchmark |
| "Low-hanging fruit" | Cliche | "Quick wins" or name them specifically |
| "Move the needle" | Cliche | Quantify the impact |
| "Robust" | Overused | Use a specific descriptor |
| "Stakeholder alignment" | Vague | "Agreement from [specific people]" |
| "Deep dive" | Cliche | "Detailed analysis of..." |
| "Holistic" | Vague | Be specific about what's included |
| "Leverage" (as verb) | Consultingese | "Use" |
| "Actionable insights" | Redundant — insights should always be actionable | "Findings" or "recommendations" |
| "World-class" | Vague overstatement | Name the specific standard |

## How to Apply This Guide

### For working documents (Steps 1-7)

Apply these principles as you write. Don't draft flabby prose with the intent to tighten later — write tight from the start. The user should never see a sentence that violates these principles.

### For the final communication output (Step 8)

After drafting the full deliverable, do a dedicated editing pass:

1. **Read every sentence.** Does every word earn its place? Cut what doesn't.
2. **Check for passive voice.** Convert to active unless there's a specific reason.
3. **Check for vague language.** Replace generalities with specifics — numbers, names, timeframes.
4. **Check slide titles / section headers.** Are they complete sentences that state the message?
5. **Read the executive summary aloud.** Does it sound like a smart person talking clearly? If it sounds like a committee wrote it, rewrite.
6. **Check the words-to-avoid list.** Search for consultingese and replace with plain language.

The executive summary gets the most scrutiny — it's the highest-visibility text in any deliverable.
