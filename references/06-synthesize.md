# Step 6: Synthesize Findings

Synthesis is not summary. Summary says "here's what we found." Synthesis says "here's what it all means together." This step takes the individual findings from Step 5 and weaves them into a coherent narrative that answers the key question.

## Interaction Model: User Provides Context and Validates the Narrative

The partner's interpretive judgment is essential here. They know the organizational context, the political landscape, and the stakeholder mindset that shapes how findings should be read. Before you synthesize, get their interpretive lens. After you synthesize, they validate whether the narrative rings true.

### INPUT Phase: Get the User's Interpretive Context

Before building the synthesis, ask the user:

1. **"Before I pull these findings together — any context or interpretive lens I should apply?"** — The user may know that certain findings will land differently with stakeholders, or that organizational history changes how to read a result.
2. **"Looking at the findings, what jumps out to you as most important?"** — The partner's emphasis tells you what to lead with.
3. **"Are there findings that surprised you? Anything that contradicts what you expected?"** — Surprises often become the most important part of the synthesis.
4. **"How do you think your stakeholders will react to these findings?"** — This shapes the framing and tone.
5. **"Are there any findings you'd want me to present cautiously or frame carefully?"** — Political sensitivity matters.

Their answers should directly shape the synthesis — what to lead with, what to emphasize, what to qualify.

## What You're Producing

A **Synthesis Document** (.md) containing:

1. **The answer to the key question** (1-2 sentences)
2. **The supporting logic** (3-5 key insights that build the case)
3. **The evidence base** (what supports each insight)
4. **Uncertainties and risks** (what could make you wrong)

## The Synthesis Process

### 1. Lay Out All Findings

Pull the key findings from Step 5 into one view. Look at them together, not individually. Ask:

- What patterns emerge across findings?
- Do any findings reinforce each other?
- Do any findings contradict each other?
- What's the overall story these findings tell?

### 2. Identify the "So What?" Chain

Good synthesis creates a chain of logic:

```
Finding A + Finding B → Insight 1
Finding C + Finding D → Insight 2
Insight 1 + Insight 2 → Answer to Key Question
```

Each level up the chain is more synthesized, more insightful, and closer to the answer.

### 3. Test the Answer

Once you have a candidate answer to the key question, stress-test it:

- **Completeness**: Does the answer address the full scope of the key question?
- **Evidence strength**: Is each supporting insight backed by solid evidence?
- **Counterarguments**: What would someone who disagrees say? Can you rebut it?
- **Actionability**: Does the answer lead to clear actions?

## Synthesis Patterns

### The Convergent Pattern
Multiple independent findings point to the same conclusion. This is the strongest case.

### The Tension Pattern
Findings point in different directions, and the synthesis resolves the tension.

### The Sequencing Pattern
Findings suggest a time-ordered set of actions rather than a single answer.

### The Conditional Pattern
The answer depends on an unresolved uncertainty.

## Output Template

```markdown
# Synthesis: [Key Question]

## The Answer
> [1-2 sentence direct answer to the key question, stated with appropriate confidence]

## Supporting Logic

### Insight 1: [Headline insight]
[2-3 sentences explaining the insight]
**Evidence**: [Key data points and findings that support this]

### Insight 2: [Headline insight]
[2-3 sentences explaining the insight]
**Evidence**: [Key data points and findings that support this]

### Insight 3: [Headline insight]
[2-3 sentences explaining the insight]
**Evidence**: [Key data points and findings that support this]

## How the Pieces Fit Together
[A paragraph or two explaining the logical chain — how the insights build on each other to support the answer. This is the "narrative thread" of the engagement.]

## What Could Make Us Wrong
| Risk / Uncertainty | Likelihood | Impact on Answer | Mitigation |
|-------------------|------------|-----------------|------------|
| [Risk 1] | [H/M/L] | [How it changes the answer] | [What to do about it] |
| [Risk 2] | [H/M/L] | [How it changes the answer] | [What to do about it] |

## Confidence Assessment
- **Overall confidence in the answer**: [High/Medium/Low]
- **Strongest part of the argument**: [Which insight is best-supported]
- **Weakest part**: [Where evidence is thinnest or assumptions most fragile]
- **What additional evidence would increase confidence**: [If we had X data, we could be more certain about Y]
```

## REVIEW Phase: User Validates the Narrative

Present the synthesis and ask for explicit validation. The user needs to confirm that the story you're telling is the right one before it becomes the basis for recommendations.

1. **Present the proposed answer**: "Based on the analysis, here's my read: [proposed answer to key question]. Does this ring true given what you know?"
2. **Validate the narrative thread**: "The story I'm seeing is: [narrative]. Does this match your intuition, or does the data mean something different to you?"
3. **Present alternative interpretations**: "One could also read this as [alternative]. Which framing feels more accurate? Or is there a third reading I'm missing?" — Always offer at least one alternative for the user to consider.
4. **Check for missing context**: "Are there organizational factors, recent events, or insider knowledge that would change how we interpret these findings?"
5. **Confidence calibration**: "I'd rate confidence as [level] because [reasons]. How does that align with your read?"
6. **Address tensions**: If findings point in different directions: "Finding A suggests X, but Finding B suggests Y. Here's how I'd reconcile these — but I want your take before we commit to this interpretation."

**The user may redirect the synthesis.** They might say:
- "The answer is right, but the framing needs to change for our audience" → Adjust the narrative framing
- "You're missing the real point — the key insight is actually..." → Restructure the synthesis around their insight
- "I don't buy Insight 2 — here's why..." → Revise or remove the challenged insight, re-present

**Do not proceed to Step 7 until the user explicitly approves the synthesis.** The synthesis is the foundation for recommendations — if it's wrong, everything downstream is wrong.

## Common Pitfalls

- **Summary masquerading as synthesis**: Listing findings sequentially is not synthesis. You need to create meaning that wasn't visible in any single finding.
- **Burying the lead**: State the answer upfront.
- **Overconfidence**: If the evidence is mixed, say so.
- **Missing the "so what?" chain**: If you can't articulate why each insight matters for the key question, the synthesis isn't complete.
- **Ignoring contradictory evidence**: Address findings that don't support the answer explicitly.
- **Locking in a narrative without validation**: The user may have critical context that changes the interpretation entirely. Always check before committing.
- **Dismissing the user's alternative reading**: If the partner sees a different story in the data, take it seriously. They may be right.
